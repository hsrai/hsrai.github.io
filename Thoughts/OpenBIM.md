# India Built UPI for Payments. Could an Open BIM Platform Transform Construction?
### From Software Dependency to Digital Sovereignty in Construction

This may sound like a crazy idea at first…

Interestingly, the idea of open digital building data is not new.

In the 1990s, the construction industry created the Industry Foundation Classes (IFC) standard through buildingSMART with the goal of enabling interoperability between BIM tools.

While IFC made important progress, most workflows today still revolve around proprietary ecosystems.

The question is whether modern cloud infrastructure, open-source tools, and collaborative software models could finally realise the open BIM vision that IFC originally imagined.

---

India is entering one of the largest infrastructure development phases in its history.

Highways, metro systems, airports, rail corridors, industrial zones, and smart cities are being built simultaneously across the country. Infrastructure programs such as the

[National Infrastructure Pipeline](National Infrastructure Pipeline)

and the

[PM GatiShakti National Master Plan](PM GatiShakti National Master Plan)

aim to transform the country’s logistics and urban landscape.

The construction sector already contributes roughly 8–9% of India’s GDP and employs tens of millions of people. Industry reports estimate that the sector’s value is already in the hundreds of billions of dollars and growing rapidly, with projections suggesting it could cross one trillion dollars in the coming decade.

At this scale, the digital systems used to design and manage infrastructure become critically important.

These digital systems are known as `Building Information Modelling (BIM)`.

But in India today, BIM is often interpreted in a very narrow way.

For many organisations, BIM effectively means working mainly with following two proprietary tools:

* Autodesk Revit
* Autodesk Navisworks

and a few more proprietary tools.

These tools are powerful and widely used globally. The issue is not their technical capability.

The issue is over-dependence on a single proprietary ecosystem.

That raises an important strategic question:

**Should India’s infrastructure future depend entirely on proprietary foreign software systems?**

---

# The Rise of BIM in India

Government agencies in India have increasingly recognised the value of BIM in improving project coordination and lifecycle management.

Taking the lead, in India, **Central Public Works Department (CPWD)** has published introductory guidelines on BIM adoption, **National Highways Authority of India (NHAI)** have begun experimenting with BIM on infrastructure projects.

These initiatives signal an important shift toward **digital construction practices**.

However, much of the BIM adoption in India has evolved around the most widely available commercial software tools. This is largely due to training availability, market penetration, and industry familiarity.

While understandable, such concentration can create **software monocultures**.

And monocultures in technology often carry long-term risks.

---

# The Hidden Risk of Software Monoculture

When an entire industry becomes dependent on a narrow set of proprietary tools, several challenges emerge.

### Vendor Lock-In

Proprietary file formats and workflows make it difficult to migrate to alternative systems later. Organisations become tied to the licensing and upgrade cycles of specific vendors.

### Skill Lock-In

Engineering graduates often learn **how to operate a particular software interface** rather than understanding the underlying principles of digital building modelling.

As a result, BIM becomes associated with specific software tools rather than an open methodology.

### Strategic Dependence

Infrastructure is a strategic national sector.

When the digital backbone of infrastructure design relies entirely on foreign proprietary platforms, questions naturally arise about **technological independence and long-term sustainability**.

India has faced similar challenges before.

In digital payments, for example, the country chose to build its own public digital infrastructure through
Unified Payments Interface.

UPI transformed the payments ecosystem and became a globally admired example of **open digital infrastructure built at national scale**.

The question is whether a similar philosophy could guide the digital future of construction.

---

# The Federated BIM Model

Most BIM workflows today follow what is known as the **federated model approach**.

In this system, different disciplines create separate models:

* architects create architectural models
* structural engineers create structural models
* mechanical and electrical engineers create services models

These models are later combined into a coordination model for review and clash detection.

The federated approach is widely practiced in the industry.

However, practitioners frequently report practical challenges:

* multiple model files circulating across teams
* version coordination issues
* delayed clash detection
* complex model management workflows

Construction industry studies often estimate that **15–20% of project costs may be lost to rework**, though the exact number varies across projects and sectors.

When coordination issues are discovered late in the design or construction stage, the consequences can be expensive.

The fundamental challenge is that **information is distributed across many files rather than managed as a single evolving dataset**.

---

# A Question the BIM Community Rarely Asks

The BIM community often debates software features, workflows, and training methods.

But one fundamental question is rarely asked:

**Why are building models still managed like large documents instead of evolving datasets?**

In most industries that deal with complex digital systems, this problem was solved long ago.

Software development moved away from exchanging large files decades ago. Instead, it adopted distributed version-control systems such as `Git` where thousands of contributors collaborate on a single evolving codebase.

Every change is tracked. Every modification is reviewable. Every version is reproducible.

By contrast, many BIM workflows still rely on exchanging large model files between teams and resolving conflicts through coordination meetings.

This raises an uncomfortable but important question:

**Is the construction industry solving a software problem using document-management approaches?**

If buildings are ultimately **data-rich digital systems**, perhaps the workflows used to manage them should begin to resemble modern data engineering rather than document exchange.

This is precisely why ideas such as Git-style BIM collaboration are starting to attract attention.

They challenge a long-standing assumption:

That building models must always behave like files.

---

# The Original Vision of Open BIM

BIM was originally intended to operate on open information standards that would allow data to move freely between software systems.

One of the most important of these standards is:

* Industry Foundation Classes

This standard was developed by buildingSMART International to enable interoperability between different BIM tools.

The idea was straightforward:

A building model should be accessible regardless of which software created it.

In practice, however, interoperability between tools is not always seamless. Engineers frequently report difficulties in exporting and importing models across different platforms.

As a result, many projects remain locked within single-vendor ecosystems.

---

# India’s Untapped Advantage: Software Talent

While the global construction industry often struggles with digital transformation, India possesses a remarkable advantage.

The country has one of the largest communities of software developers in the world.

Millions of engineers work daily with technologies such as:

* distributed version control
* open-source software
* cloud computing
* collaborative development workflows

These are precisely the technologies that power modern software innovation.

This raises an intriguing possibility.

Could the **next generation of BIM workflows borrow ideas from software development itself?**

> If buildings (in BIM) are digital systems, their workflows should evolve like software - transparent, versioned, and collaborative.

---

# Learning from Software Engineering

Software development solved large-scale collaboration problems decades ago.

Thousands of developers now work together on massive codebases using version-control systems like:

* Git

Git allows contributors to:

* track every change made to a project
* collaborate safely without overwriting each other’s work
* review changes before they become part of the main system

This process has become the backbone of global open-source development.

Some researchers and developers are now exploring whether similar concepts could apply to BIM.

---

# The Idea of Git-Based BIM

Imagine a construction project where the building model evolves in a **version-controlled environment**.

Instead of exchanging dozens of separate model files, the project data would live in a shared repository.

Engineers could:

* propose design changes
* review modifications transparently
* automatically check clashes and rules before merging changes

Such a system could bring greater transparency and traceability to project coordination.

This concept — sometimes described informally as **Git-based BIM workflows** — is still emerging.

But the technological pieces already exist.

Open-source tools such as:

* IfcOpenShell
* BlenderBIM
* FreeCAD

are beginning to explore open BIM ecosystems built around standards rather than proprietary platforms.

These tools do not yet implement a full Git-style BIM workflow, but they demonstrate that **open and collaborative BIM infrastructures are technically possible**.

---

# Why This Matters for India

India stands at a unique crossroads.

On one path lies continued dependence on existing proprietary ecosystems.

On the other lies the opportunity to **experiment and innovate with open digital infrastructure for construction**.

The second path is not easy.

It requires research, experimentation, and new tools.

But it could also produce significant benefits:

* development of domestic BIM technology
* creation of high-value engineering and software jobs
* reduced long-term dependence on proprietary systems
* stronger integration between software engineering and civil engineering

Most importantly, it could allow India to **shape the future of construction technology rather than merely adopt it**.

---

# A Balanced Approach

This discussion is not about rejecting global software tools.

Revit, Navisworks, and similar platforms will remain important parts of the BIM ecosystem for years to come.

The real issue is **diversity and innovation**.

A healthy technological ecosystem benefits from:

* open standards
* multiple competing tools
* active research and experimentation

Encouraging open BIM platforms and collaborative workflows can strengthen the entire industry.

---

## What an Open BIM Platform for India Could Look Like

If India were to experiment with an open digital platform for construction, it would not need to start from scratch. Many foundational technologies already exist.

A conceptual architecture might include several layers.

### 1. Open Data Layer

The foundation would rely on open standards such as
Industry Foundation Classes
to ensure that building information remains accessible regardless of the software used to create it.

This would protect long-term project data and prevent vendor lock-in.

### 2. Open Modeling Tools

A growing ecosystem of open-source tools already works with IFC data, including:

- FreeCAD
- BlenderBIM

These tools demonstrate that BIM authoring environments do not have to be tied to proprietary platforms.

### 3. Collaborative Data Infrastructure

Project data could be stored and managed in distributed repositories using technologies inspired by
Git.

Such systems allow large teams to collaborate on complex digital assets while tracking every change.

Adapted carefully for building models, similar workflows could bring transparency and traceability to construction projects.

### 4. Automated Engineering Checks

Modern software development relies heavily on automated testing.

A future BIM platform could similarly run automated checks for:

- clash detection
- building code compliance
- quantity verification
- design rule validation

before changes are accepted into the main model.

### 5. Open Innovation Layer

Perhaps the most powerful feature of an open BIM platform would be the ability for startups, universities, and developers to build new tools on top of it.

Just as UPI enabled innovation in financial technology, an open BIM platform could enable innovation in construction technology.

India is building infrastructure at an unprecedented pace.

The digital tools used to design, coordinate, and manage that infrastructure will shape the industry for decades.

The question is not whether BIM will be used.

It already is.

The real question is more fundamental:

**Will India only use BIM technologies created elsewhere — or will it help invent the next generation of BIM workflows itself?**

India has already demonstrated its ability to build world-class digital infrastructure in sectors such as payments and identity.

There is no reason the same spirit of innovation cannot transform construction technology.

The opportunity is clear.

**India could become not just a consumer of BIM technology, but a creator of the next generation of digital construction systems.**


> If India could build world-class digital public infrastructure for payments, perhaps the next frontier is open digital infrastructure for construction.
